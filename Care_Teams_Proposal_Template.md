## Executive Summary / Abstract

### English Abstract (Recommended 400-500 words)

Hong Kong's 2022 Policy Address introduced the Care Teams initiative as a cornerstone of district governance reform, establishing 455 teams across 18 districts to strengthen community support networks. Despite this significant policy investment, limited empirical research exists on public perceptions of welfare deservingness in community care contexts and how these perceptions align with government policy priorities.

This study aims to examine: (1) public preferences regarding who deserves community care services; (2) the alignment between public perceptions and government agenda-setting for Care Teams; and (3) the factors influencing deservingness perceptions using the CARIN Framework (Control, Attitude, Reciprocity, Identity, Need).

Adopting a mixed-methods approach, this research integrates quantitative survey research with qualitative investigations. The quantitative component involves a territory-wide survey of 1,800 Hong Kong residents using stratified random sampling, incorporating vignette experiments to assess deservingness judgments across different recipient profiles. The qualitative component includes 8-10 focus groups with diverse stakeholders (residents, Care Team members, district councillors) and 20-30 in-depth interviews with key informants.

Data analysis will employ Structural Equation Modelling (SEM) and Hierarchical Linear Modelling (HLM) for quantitative data, and thematic analysis for qualitative data. The theoretical framework combines the CARIN Framework for welfare deservingness with Agenda-Setting Theory to examine the interplay between public opinion and policy priorities.

Expected outcomes include: (1) empirical evidence on Hong Kong residents' deservingness perceptions; (2) assessment of agenda alignment between public and government priorities; (3) validated measurement instruments for future research; and (4) evidence-based policy recommendations for optimizing Care Teams deployment and resource allocation.

This research addresses a critical knowledge gap at the intersection of welfare policy and district governance, providing actionable insights for enhancing the effectiveness of community care initiatives in Hong Kong.

**Keywords:** Care Teams; welfare deservingness; CARIN Framework; agenda-setting; community care; Hong Kong

## 1. Background and Rationale

### 1.1 Policy Context

The 2022 Policy Address marked a significant shift in Hong Kong's approach to district governance, introducing Care Teams (關愛隊) as a key mechanism for strengthening community support networks. As of [current date], 455 Care Teams have been established across all 18 districts, representing a substantial investment in community-level welfare delivery.

Care Teams are tasked with:
- Identifying and supporting vulnerable residents
- Coordinating community resources
- Facilitating communication between residents and government
- Responding to community needs and emergencies

Despite this significant policy initiative, several critical questions remain unanswered:
- How do Hong Kong residents perceive who deserves community care services?
- To what extent do public preferences align with government priorities?
- What factors influence deservingness judgments in the Hong Kong context?

### 1.2 Research Gap

Existing research on welfare deservingness has predominantly focused on Western contexts, with limited application to Hong Kong's unique socio-political environment. The CARIN Framework (van Oorschot, 2000, 2006), while widely validated internationally, has not been systematically applied to community care contexts in Hong Kong.

Furthermore, the relationship between public opinion and policy agenda-setting (McCombs & Shaw, 1972) in Hong Kong's welfare domain remains underexplored, particularly following recent governance reforms.

### 1.3 Significance

This research addresses these gaps by:
1. Providing the first systematic study of welfare deservingness perceptions in Hong Kong's community care context
2. Examining agenda alignment between public preferences and government priorities
3. Generating evidence-based recommendations for Care Teams optimization
4. Contributing to theoretical development by testing the CARIN Framework in a new cultural context

## 3. Theoretical Framework

### 3.1 CARIN Framework for Welfare Deservingness

The CARIN Framework (van Oorschot, 2000, 2006) identifies five criteria that influence public judgments of welfare deservingness:

| Criterion | Definition | Application to Care Teams |
|-----------|------------|---------------------------|
| Control | Perceived responsibility for one's situation | Did the person cause their own need? |
| Attitude | Perceived gratitude and compliance | Is the person appreciative of help? |
| Reciprocity | Past and potential contributions | Has the person contributed to society? |
| Identity | Social proximity and similarity | Is the person "one of us"? |
| Need | Severity and urgency of situation | How desperate is the person's situation? |

### 3.2 Agenda-Setting Theory

Agenda-Setting Theory (McCombs & Shaw, 1972) posits that media and policy actors influence public perceptions of issue importance. This study extends this framework to examine:
- Government agenda-setting through Care Teams priorities
- Public agenda formation regarding community care
- Alignment/misalignment between these agendas

## 4. Research Methodology

### 4.1 Research Design

This study employs a mixed-methods sequential explanatory design, combining:
- Phase 1: Quantitative survey with vignette experiments (N=1,800)
- Phase 2: Qualitative focus groups and interviews (N=80-100 participants)

### 4.2 Quantitative Component

#### 4.2.1 Sampling Strategy

**Target Population:** Hong Kong residents aged 18 or above

**Sampling Method:** Stratified random sampling by district and demographic characteristics

**Sample Size:** 1,800 respondents
- Justification: Power analysis indicates N=1,800 provides 95% confidence level with ±2.3% margin of error; sufficient for SEM analysis (minimum 10:1 ratio of cases to parameters)

**Stratification:**
- 18 districts (proportional to population)
- Age groups (18-29, 30-44, 45-59, 60+)
- Gender (male/female)

#### 4.2.2 Survey Instrument

The questionnaire comprises five sections:

**Section A: Deservingness Perceptions**
- Adapted CARIN scale items (van Oorschot, 2006)
- 25 items measuring five CARIN dimensions
- 7-point Likert scale (1=Strongly Disagree to 7=Strongly Agree)

**Section B: Vignette Experiments**
- 8 vignettes describing potential Care Teams recipients
- Systematically varied characteristics (age, health status, family situation, employment, contribution history)
- Respondents rate deservingness of each vignette character

**Section C: Care Teams Awareness and Attitudes**
- Awareness of Care Teams existence and functions
- Perceived effectiveness
- Personal experience with Care Teams

**Section D: Policy Priorities**
- Ranking of community care priorities
- Comparison with government-stated priorities
- Support for various Care Teams activities

**Section E: Demographics**
- Age, gender, education, income
- District of residence
- Housing type
- Political orientation

#### 4.2.3 Data Collection

**Method:** Computer-Assisted Telephone Interviewing (CATI) + Online survey option

**Duration:** Approximately 25-30 minutes per respondent

**Timeline:** Months 6-10

**Quality Control:**
- Pilot testing with 50 respondents
- Interviewer training
- Random callback verification (10% of sample)

#### 4.2.4 Quantitative Analysis

**Descriptive Analysis:**
- Frequency distributions
- Cross-tabulations by demographics and district

**Inferential Analysis:**
- Confirmatory Factor Analysis (CFA) for CARIN scale validation
- Structural Equation Modelling (SEM) for testing theoretical model
- Hierarchical Linear Modelling (HLM) for district-level effects
- Regression analysis for vignette experiments

**Software:** SPSS 28, Mplus 8, HLM 8

### 4.3 Qualitative Component

#### 4.3.1 Focus Groups

**Number:** 8-10 focus groups

**Composition:**
- 2 groups: General residents (mixed demographics)
- 2 groups: Elderly residents (65+)
- 2 groups: Care Team members
- 2 groups: District councillors/community leaders
- 2 groups: Social workers and NGO representatives

**Size:** 6-8 participants per group

**Duration:** 90-120 minutes

**Topics:**
- Perceptions of community care needs
- Views on Care Teams effectiveness
- Criteria for deserving assistance
- Suggestions for improvement

#### 4.3.2 In-depth Interviews

**Number:** 20-30 interviews

**Participants:**
- 10 Care Team coordinators
- 5 District Officers
- 5 NGO leaders
- 5-10 Care Teams service recipients

**Duration:** 45-60 minutes

**Topics:**
- Implementation experiences
- Targeting decisions
- Challenges and successes
- Policy recommendations

#### 4.3.3 Qualitative Analysis

**Approach:** Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006)

**Process:**
1. Transcription and translation (where needed)
2. Familiarization with data
3. Initial coding
4. Theme development
5. Theme review and refinement
6. Final analysis and reporting

**Software:** NVivo 14

**Rigor:**
- Dual coding by two researchers
- Member checking with selected participants
- Peer debriefing
- Audit trail documentation

### 4.4 Integration of Findings

Quantitative and qualitative findings will be integrated through:
- Joint display tables comparing survey results with interview themes
- Explanatory sequential analysis (qualitative explaining quantitative patterns)
- Triangulation of findings across methods

**Ethics Approval:** [Institution] Survey and Behavioural Research Ethics Committee

**Informed Consent:** Written consent for interviews; verbal consent for telephone surveys

**Confidentiality:**
