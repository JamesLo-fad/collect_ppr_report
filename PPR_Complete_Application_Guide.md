### REAL EXAMPLE: Abstract from 2016.A4.022.16C (Palliative Care Study)

**English Abstract (Actual text from report):**

"Hong Kong is facing an ageing population with increasing demand for palliative and end-of-life care services. Despite the growing needs, the development of such services has been slow and fragmented. This study aims to examine the current state of palliative and end-of-life care services in Hong Kong, identify the strengths, weaknesses, opportunities and threats (SWOT) in service development, and explore the barriers and facilitators to service enhancement.

A qualitative study design was adopted. Semi-structured interviews were conducted with 67 participants, including patients, family carers, health professionals, administrators, frontline care staff and others. Thematic analysis was used to analyse the data.

The findings revealed that while Hong Kong has high-quality palliative care services with committed staff, there are significant weaknesses including limited coverage, poor accessibility, fragmentation of care, and delayed referral. Opportunities for development include leaders' support and changes in demographic patterns. Threats include curative-oriented approach, heavy clinical workload, and inadequate community support.

Based on the findings, policy recommendations are proposed including establishing an overall policy framework, ensuring stable financial support, promoting public education, and enhancing community and home care support. The study provides evidence-based recommendations for the development of palliative and end-of-life care services in Hong Kong."

### REAL EXAMPLE: Abstract from 2020.A3.025.20C (Cooling Measures Study)

**English Abstract (Actual text from report):**

"Since the Global Financial Crisis, Hong Kong has witnessed house prices surge from one record high to the next. In an attempt to rein in the overheated housing market, the Hong Kong government rolled out 12 rounds of interventions (cooling measures) from October 2009 to May 2017, which included tightening the mortgage supply and imposing various stamp duties. Despite intensive debate among market observers, no consensus has been reached on whether the measures have been successful. There has also been growing concern over the unintended consequences of the measures.

This project uses comprehensive transaction data covering over 90% of private residential transactions from January 2008 to October 2018. Using regression discontinuity design, we examine the 'net effect' of each round of cooling measures on residential prices and transaction volumes.

Research results indicate that mortgage-tightening measures effectively reduced residential prices and transaction volumes in the short term. However, tax-related measures, while more effective in curbing transaction volumes, also triggered price increases across the residential market. The findings provide a comprehensive assessment of the short-term impacts of the twelve rounds of cooling measures, offering reference for policymakers in reviewing current measures and designing future housing policies."

**REAL EXAMPLE from 2015.A4.008.15C (Diabetes Care PPP):**

```
(i) Existing Policy and Limitations

The Hospital Authority (HA) currently provides over 90% of inpatient care and
70% of outpatient care in Hong Kong. With an estimated 600,000 adults having
diabetes, approximately 400,000 are followed up in the HA setting. However,
the current system faces significant challenges:

- HA employs 45% of registered doctors but manages 90% of diabetes patients
- Huge differences in drug acquisition costs between public and private sectors
- Lack of support for comprehensive assessment outside HA setting
- Fee-for-service model excludes private doctors from providing quality care

(ii) Necessity of Proposed Study

Quality diabetes care can save lives and money through stable doctor-patient
relationships, patient education, long-term medications, and regular monitoring.
The JADE Program has demonstrated 20-50% lower hospitalization rates compared
to standard care. This study will provide evidence for scaling up this model
through public-private partnership.

(iii) Translation to Policy Recommendations

Key deliverables include:
- Validated Chinese Diabetes Outcome Model for projecting clinical outcomes
- Cost-benefit analysis of PPP implementation
- Policy brief for government stakeholders
- Implementation guidelines for healthcare providers

Quantifiable indicators:
- Projected reduction in hospitalizations
- Estimated cost savings over 15-year period
- Number of patients benefiting from PPP scheme

(iv) Feasibility Testing

- Stakeholder consultations with HA, Bureau, and insurers
- Cost-effectiveness analysis using simulation model
- Pilot implementation data from existing JADE centres
- Feedback collection from patients and care providers
```

**REAL EXAMPLE from 2013.A3.002.13A (Optional Retirement):**

```
1. Policy Context

The population in Hong Kong is ageing at an unprecedented speed, with a
projection in 2041 that the population of 65-years-old or over will increase
to over two million. Many organizations have set the retirement age for their
employees at 60, which wastes precious skills and manpower unnecessarily.

Previous studies have shown that Hong Kong employees have the willingness to
work beyond the current retirement age. With the ever-growing life expectancy,
insufficient pension generated during a working life leads to a tremendous
economic cost of retirement.

2. Literature Review

2.1 Regional Experience on Retirement Arrangements

European Union: The EU has implemented various measures including extending
retirement age, promoting flexible working arrangements, and providing
incentives for employers to retain older workers.

Finland: Implemented comprehensive pension reform with flexible retirement
options between ages 63-68.

United Kingdom: Centre for Diversity Policy Research and Practice (2011)
designed an optional retirement model with four options:
- Option 1: Continue working on same contract
- Option 2: Phased retirement arrangements
- Option 3: Post-retirement contract
- Option 4: Consultant or freelancer

Singapore: Retirement and Re-employment Act (RRA) requires employers to
offer re-employment to eligible employees up to age 65.

2.2 Hong Kong Context

In the public sector, normal retirement age is 55-60 depending on pension
scheme. In the private sector, most organizations set retirement age at 60.
Hong Kong Institute of Asia-Pacific Studies (2014) indicated 75% respondents
have shown intention to extend their working life.

3. Research Gap

Limited local studies exist on optional retirement implementation. This study
addresses this gap by examining preferences and feasibility from both employer
and employee perspectives.
```

**REAL EXAMPLE from 2015.A1.023.15C (Youth Violence & Social Capital):**

```
RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

Study Design
This study employs a mixed-methods approach combining quantitative survey
research with qualitative focus group discussions.

Quantitative Component

Sampling:
- Target population: Chinese-speaking youths aged 16 to 24 years
- Sampling method: Simple random sampling from residential addresses
- Sample size: 2,316 youths (2,176 from residences, 140 from youth services)
- Response rate: 55.0%

Instrument:
The survey questionnaire measures:
- Violence involvement (perpetration, victimization, witnessing)
- Social capital indicators (peer network, elder network, linchpin network)
- Policy exposure (youth services, volunteer service invitations)
- Demographics

Data Collection:
- Method: Face-to-face interviews at residences and youth service units
- Duration: September 2014 to May 2016
- Quality control: Working meetings with youth service units

Analysis:
- Regression analysis to examine relationships between variables
- Standardized effects (β) reported for all relationships
- Significance level: p < 0.05

Qualitative Component

Participants:
- 40 youths engaged in focus groups
- Purposive sampling to ensure diversity

Data Collection:
- Semi-structured focus group discussions
- Topics: violence experiences, social capital, policy influences

Analysis:
- Grounded theory approach
- Thematic analysis to substantiate statistical findings
```

**REAL EXAMPLE from 2016.A4.022.16C (Palliative Care):**

```
RESEARCH METHODOLOGY

3.1 Study Design
A qualitative study design was adopted to explore the perspectives of
multiple stakeholders on palliative and end-of-life care services.

3.2 Participants
67 participants were recruited, including:
- Patients receiving palliative care
- Family carers
- Health professionals (doctors, nurses)
- Administrators
- Frontline care staff
- Others (social workers, volunteers)

3.3 Sampling Strategy
Purposive sampling was used to ensure diversity in:
- Types of participants
- Care settings (hospital, community, residential)
- Geographic locations

3.4 Interview
Semi-structured interviews were conducted using interview guides
developed based on literature review and expert consultation.

Interview topics included:
- Current state of services
- Strengths and achievements
- Challenges and barriers
- Suggestions for improvement

3.5 Data Collection Procedures
- Interviews conducted between [dates]
- Duration: 45-90 minutes per interview
- Audio-recorded with participant consent
- Transcribed verbatim

3.6 Data Analysis
Thematic analysis was employed following Braun and Clarke's (2006)
six-phase approach:
1. Familiarization with data
2. Generating initial codes
3. Searching for themes
4. Reviewing themes
5. Defining and naming themes
6. Producing the report

3.7 Rigor
- Member checking with selected participants
- Peer debriefing among research team
- Audit trail documentation

3.8 Study Limitations
- Qualitative design limits generalizability
- Participants may not represent all stakeholder views
- Self-selection bias possible
```
